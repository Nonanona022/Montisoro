Part A · For the user

Six steps to
your cost picture.

The calculator is a guided wizard. You fill in six short steps; each field is editable via a slider or by typing the exact number. The result is a first indication — not to capture everything exactly, but to make visible what is often underestimated.

01

Organisation

Number of employees (FTE, part-timers pro rata) and the blue-collar / white-collar split. This sets the headcount basis for the whole model.

02

Average gross salary

Gross salary per year, incl. holiday pay, year-end bonus and average bonus — excluding employer social security (added separately by the engine).

03

Recurring costs

Fixed costs alongside guaranteed salary: benefits in kind, group, hospitalisation and other insurance, company car — each assignable to blue-collar, white-collar or both.

04

Replacement costs

The share of direct costs added to get the work done anyway: temp staff, temporary replacement, extra hiring or overtime. Expressed as a %.

05

Organisational impact

The non-invoiced cost: productivity and quality loss, management time, knowledge loss, delays, reintegration. As a % of direct salary cost (default ± 50%).

06

Absence profile

Net working days per year (± 220) and absence per category — short (≤ 1 mo), medium (1 mo–1 yr) and long-term (> 1 yr) — separately for blue- and white-collar.

Editable & transparent: every input field shows a pencil icon — drag the slider or type the exact number. Light validation flags missing core data (number of employees and gross salary).

Reading your report

Six insights into your figure.

The report translates your input into six readable outcomes. Two cost concepts are central — and the difference between them matters.

Employer cost

The headline figure: the invoiceable annual cost of absence — direct salary cost + recurring costs + replacement. This often appears (partly) in the books.

Total cost

The employer cost plus the estimated organisational impact — the non-invoiced cost that is rarely visible anywhere. Usually the figure that surprises.

Benchmark

Your absence rate against the Belgian sector average, per category and in total — so you immediately see where you deviate.

Risk level

Low, Average, Elevated or High — set by how far your absence sits above or below the sector average, with a short explanation.

Savings scenarios

What a reduction of 0.5 · 1 · 2 percentage points of absence would save you annually — translated linearly into euros.

Priorities

Three concrete focus points, automatically derived from the category where you deviate most from the benchmark.

The report also shows the FTE equivalent: all lost working days combined, converted into full-time employees. Often the most tangible number.

The calculation model

From input to cost.

The model is fully deterministic and transparent. Below is the core calculation, step by step, with the exact formulas as the engine applies them.

The chain, in order
// cost of one lost working day
cost/day = ( gross salary ÷ working days ) × 1.30 (employer SSC +30%)
// lost days, per group, summed
lost days = employees × working days × ( absence% ÷ 100 )
// direct salary cost of absence
salary cost = lost days × cost/day
replacement cost = salary cost × ( replacement factor ÷ 100 )
org. impact = salary cost × ( org. factor ÷ 100 )
// the headline + the total
employer cost = salary cost + recurring costs + replacement
total cost = employer cost + org. impact

Derived: FTE equivalent = lost days ÷ working days · cost per employee = employer cost ÷ FTE · recurring costs = benefits + insurance, distributed pro rata across the lost days.

Savings & priorities

From figure to action.

Savings scenarios

Linear model: the cost is proportional to the absence rate. A reduction of X percentage points yields employer cost × (X ÷ total absence%), capped so the reduction is never larger than current absence.

Scenario 1 — modest
− 0.5 percentage points
Scenario 2 — targeted
− 1.0 percentage points
Scenario 3 — ambitious
− 2.0 percentage points
Priorities — rule-based

The engine ranks the three absence categories by their deviation from the sector benchmark and selects the matching recommendation. If no category deviates notably, it supplements with generic priorities (make data visible, anchor policy).

Short

Frequent absence

Strengthen managers in return-to-work and absence conversations; surface patterns early.

Medium

Case management

Structured case management so cases do not evolve into long-term absence.

Long-term

Reintegration

External direction on protracted cases to speed up return and control the heaviest cost driver.

The report closes with three concrete priorities — not a scorecard, but a starting point for the conversation.

Legal context

Solidarity contribution (RIT 3.0): in 2026 the employer pays 30% of the sickness benefit amount in months 2 and 3 of incapacity; in 2027 extended to months 4 and 5. The calculator keeps gross salary excluding employer social security; that is added separately (×1.30).

Disclaimer: the calculator provides a substantiated indication based on your input and the Belgian sector average — not an audit or accounting statement. The actual cost depends on organisation-specific factors. Intended to start the right conversation, not to capture everything exactly.
