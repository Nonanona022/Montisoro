Foreword

The bill that's on its way

In the fourth quarter of 2026, the NSSO will send you a debit notice you have never seen before.

It's the solidarity contribution: 30% of the sickness benefit of every long-term ill employee, during the second and third month of their incapacity for work. It applies from 50 employees, it has been running since 1 January, and it is calculated day by day.

But the contribution is not the most expensive thing about RIT 3.0.

The most expensive thing is the fine. From 20 employees you have, since this year, been obliged to start a reintegration pathway within six months. If you don't, a level 2 sanction follows — multiplied by the number of employees involved.

Eight files in which nothing happened can add up to tens of thousands of euros. Plus the contribution that has been running in the meantime. Plus the absence cost that no one counted.

The way out
The contribution lapses entirely the moment your employee resumes part-time work.
During a recognised progressive return to work, the contribution is not due. Not reduced — gone. That is not a loophole; it is the very purpose of the entire measure.

This guide explains what is expected of you, when, from which threshold, and what it costs if you don't act. Twelve pages. You can tick them off.

Section 1

The reintegration pathway in brief

What it is

The reintegration pathway (RIT) is the legal procedure used to examine whether an incapacitated employee can resume work — their own work, adapted work, or other work. It is not a medical procedure and not a dismissal procedure. It is an examination of possibilities.

The purpose

To bring as many people as possible back to work, as early as possible. The legislator sees long-term incapacity for work as an outcome that is often avoidable, and since 2026 expects the employer to actively cooperate.

Who is involved

The RIT is the procedure. RIT 3.0 is what is expected of you since 1 January 2026 — and for the first time there is a fine if you don't do it.
Section 2 · the heart of the guide

What changes in 3.0?

The legal basis

Two texts, both in the Belgian Official Gazette on 30 December 2025: the Act of 19 December 2025 implementing a strengthened return-to-work policy, and the Royal Decree of 17 December 2025 amending the code on well-being at work. The Royal Decree entered into force on 1 January 2026.

⚠ Important transitional rule

The new rules apply to incapacities for work that start from 1 January 2026. Pathways that were already running before that date remain under the old rules (RIT 2.0) for their full duration.

So for the time being you are working with two regimes side by side. Know for each ongoing file which regime it falls under.

The seven key changes

1

The employer can now start the pathway from day one

Previously only after three months. Now from the first day, subject to the employee's consent. The most important change no one talks about — it turns reintegration from something you undergo into something you steer.

2

There is now a preventive pathway — without illness

An employee at risk of dropping out can ask for a workplace adaptation or adapted work to be examined. They do not need to be incapacitated for work to do so.

3

From 20 employees: starting is mandatory

Once work potential is established, the employer must start an RIT within six months of the start of the incapacity for work.

4

There are now sanctions. On both sides.

For the first time. See section 4.

5

The employee must cooperate

Summons are issued by registered letter. Two missed summons without valid reason are reported to the advising physician; the pathway can be terminated, with consequences for the right to benefits.

6

Medical force majeure: from 9 to 6 months

The minimum period for starting the procedure to establish definitive incapacity has been shortened from nine to six months.

7

Communication via TRIO

Exchange between the occupational physician, the treating physician and the advising physician runs via the TRIO platform. The occupational physician takes into account a questionnaire completed by the employee.

What does NOT change

The broad lines of RIT 2.0 remain. The decoupling of the reintegration pathway from termination for medical force majeure — the key change of 2022 — remains. An RIT is still not a dismissal procedure. That is important to repeat internally: the greatest resistance comes from employees who think the pathway is a prelude to dismissal. It is not, and has not been since 2022.

Two things that change in your favour

Not everything becomes more expensive. Two changes to guaranteed salary, since 1 January 2026:

The relapse period goes from 14 days to 8 weeks.

If an employee relapses within eight weeks due to the same illness or the same accident, you owe no new guaranteed salary. (The balance of the earlier guaranteed salary continues to run; for a demonstrably different condition it remains due.)

Neutralisation during progressive return to work becomes complete.

If an employee drops out entirely during a progressive return to work, no guaranteed salary is due — regardless of how long the part-time resumption has been running. Both rules apply to incapacities starting from 1 January 2026.

Your obligations, step by step

First: which threshold applies to you?

This is where most organisations go wrong. There are three thresholds, and they are not the same.

EVERY EMPLOYER
→ Contact procedure mandatory in the work rules
From 20 employees
→ Mandatory to start an RIT within 6 months where work potential is established · level 2 sanction for non-compliance
From 50 employees

An organisation of 30 people pays no solidarity contribution — but does risk a fine if it does not start a reintegration pathway.

That distinction is structurally overlooked. It is also why "we're too small for all this" is almost always a wrong conclusion.

Section 4 · for the CEO and the CFO

What does it cost, and what does doing nothing cost?

Part A — What it costs you

1. The solidarity contribution (50+ employees)

The formula: 30% of the sickness benefit your employee receives from the health insurance fund, during the second and third month of the incapacity for work. Day by day from day 31.

  • An average of 50+ employees
  • Employee incapacitated for work for more than 30 days
  • Between 18 and 54 years old at the start of the incapacity
  • At least one month in service

Collection runs via the NSSO, in the third quarter after the quarter in which the illness began. The first debit notice comes in Q4 2026.

2. The guaranteed salary

Month 1 remains entirely at your expense. That does not change.

3. Everything that isn't on a statement

The solidarity contribution is the most visible cost of long-term absence. It is also the smallest. What else continues to run while your employee is at home:

  • Group insurance, hospitalisation insurance, company car, benefits in kind
  • Replacement costs: temp workers, overtime, temporary hiring
  • Loss of productivity among the colleagues who absorb it
  • Management time · loss of knowledge · loss of planning, quality and delivery

Taken together, the real cost of a single day of absence runs up to €266.

The solidarity contribution makes visible what you were already paying. That is, however irritating, the greatest service the legislator has done you.

Part B — What doing nothing costs you

An employer with 20 or more employees who does not ask the occupational physician to start an RIT for an incapacitated employee with established work potential, within six months, risks a level 2 sanction under the Social Criminal Code.

Base amount× surcharges (×10 since 1 Feb 2026)
Criminal fine€50 – €500€500 – €5,000
Administrative fine€25 – €250€250 – €2,500

In case of criminal prosecution, the administrative fine lapses. It is one or the other.

The fine is multiplied by the number of employees involved. Not per infringement — per employee.
Worked example
An organisation of 200 people. 8 long-term ill employees with established work potential. Not a single reintegration pathway started.
Administrative:  8 × up to €2,500  =  up to €20,000
Criminal:  8 × up to €5,000  =  up to €40,000

+ the solidarity contribution that has been running in the meantime
+ the absence cost that no one counted
Mind the date

On 1 February 2026, the surcharges were raised from ×8 to ×10. All fines in the Social Criminal Code became 25% more expensive over the course of the same year.

On the employee's side

  • The sanction for non-attendance at the Return-to-Work coordinator went from 2.5% to 10% of the benefit.
  • Unjustified absence at physical contact moments → risk of the benefit being stopped.
  • Two missed summons from the occupational physician → report to the advising physician.

That does not relieve you of your own obligation. The sanction on the employee is no defence for the employer.

The way out written into the law

The solidarity contribution lapses entirely the moment your employee resumes work part-time.

During a period of progressive return to work, recognised by the advising physician, the contribution is not due. Not reduced — gone. That is not a loophole; it is the very purpose of the entire measure.

Section 5

Common mistakes

Sample data · still to be written from Storm + Laurence

This is how this section will read once the file data is processed — the mistakes and figures below are an example, not a verified finding.

Mistake 1

Waiting until the three months are "full"

The old reflex: only take action after three months of illness. Since 2026 you may act from day one — and it's precisely those first weeks that determine the outcome. In files where there was contact in the first two weeks, the chance of returning to work is noticeably higher than where contact only came after month two.

What to do instead: set one internal moment, in week 1, where you decide whether to start early.
Mistake 2

No contact, out of fear of being "wrong"

Managers don't dare to call — afraid of saying the wrong thing or coming across as "too harsh". Result: the employee feels forgotten, and that is the hardest situation to reintegrate from.

What to do instead: give managers a simple calling framework — human contact, no medical questions.
Mistake 3

The employee thinks the pathway means dismissal

The most common reason a pathway is refused. It's not true — an RIT has been decoupled from medical force majeure since 2022 — but the perception persists stubbornly. Left unaddressed, it blocks the entire pathway.

What to do instead: name it explicitly, in the first conversation. "This is not a dismissal procedure" — and explain why.
Mistake 4

Progressive return to work starts, but no one follows up

The resumption is agreed and then let go. It stalls at the first setback — and then the solidarity contribution comes back after all. The very instrument that makes the contribution lapse fails through a lack of follow-up.

What to do instead: appoint one person who follows up the resumption week by week.
