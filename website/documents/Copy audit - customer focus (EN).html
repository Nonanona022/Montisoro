Internal copy audit · not published

Does the site talk about us,
or about you?

A page-by-page review of every visible line of copy on the Montisoro website, against one question: is the visitor at the centre, or Montisoro? Each finding comes with a concrete rewrite — Before → After — and the behavioural rationale. Nothing on the site has been changed; these are proposals. (English mirror of the Dutch audit.)

Prepared6 July 2026
Scope10 live pages + shared CTAs
GoalFully customer-focused · min. 9.5/10

The principle behind this audit

The visitor is the hero. Montisoro is the guide.

Visitors don't buy a company — they buy a better version of their own situation. Whoever lands on the site (leadership, HR facing complex absence) doesn't think “what a strong company”, but “does this company understand my problem, and will I be better off here?”. The strongest B2B copy casts the client in the lead role and positions the brand as the guide that gets them there — not as the hero telling its own story.

In practice that comes down to three reflexes, applicable to every sentence:

Subject = the clientLet “you / your organization” perform the verb, not “Montisoro brings / builds / makes”.
Headline = the problem or the gainTitles name the visitor's pain or outcome, not the brand's name or foundation.
Proof may be about usNumbers, references and expertise show authority — that's exactly where “we” is fitting and credible.

The base is strong: Calculator, Fit Check and Contact are already model examples of customer-focused copy. 1 · Home

7.0 / 10 A strong, problem-led hero opening and an excellent calculator block — but three anchors pull attention to the brand instead of the visitor.
1

Hero · subtitle

From “Montisoro brings together” to “so it's never left to chance for you”

The first sentence is perfectly customer-focused (the cost hits the visitor). The second switches to Montisoro as subject: the company “brings together”. The visitor drops out of the very sentence meant to carry the promise.

Before

“Every day an employee is absent costs up to €266. Montisoro brings together care, expertise and technology so that a lasting return to work is never left to chance.”

After

“Every day an employee is absent costs you up to €266. With care, expertise and technology in one system, a lasting return to work is no longer left to chance — it becomes structure.”

Why stronger: the promise is now about the visitor's outcome (“becomes structure”) rather than what Montisoro does. “Costs you” personalises the pain; the instruments remain as means, not lead role.

2

“Strengths” section · eyebrow + title

“Why Montisoro” is an about-ourselves heading

Both the eyebrow (“Why Montisoro”) and the title (“Where existing roles stop, Montisoro begins”) make the brand the subject. The visitor has to translate it to their own situation themselves.

Before

Eyebrow: “Why Montisoro” — Title: “Where existing roles stop, Montisoro begins.”

After

Eyebrow: “Why this works” — Title: “Where your existing roles stop, the real work begins.”

Why stronger: the heading now names the gap the visitor already feels (“your existing roles stop”) — a limit they recognise — instead of a brand claim. Montisoro's positioning as the solution stays implicit and gains credibility.

3

“Strengths” section · pillar 01

“Montisoro brings these perspectives together”

A pure we-do sentence. The valuable part (one well-founded approach) is framed as something Montisoro does, not something the visitor gets.

Before

“From fragmentation to direction — Absence affects people, work and the organization. Montisoro brings these perspectives together into one well-founded approach.”

After

“From fragmentation to direction — Absence affects people, work and the organization at once. You get those perspectives back in one well-founded approach.”

Why stronger: “You get … back” makes the visitor the recipient of the gain. The emphasis shifts from the brand's performance to the result inside the reader's organization.

4

“Foundation” section · eyebrow + intro

“The Montisoro Foundation” is about the brand, not the gain

The heading and intro describe how Montisoro is built (one layer, three instruments, eight years of expertise). That's classic “look how we're put together” copy. The visitor reads what the brand is, not what it does for them.

Before

Eyebrow: “The Montisoro Foundation” — “Organizations that care for their people perform better. One cultural layer, carried by three instruments, built on eight years of expertise…”

After

Eyebrow: “What you can build on” — “Organizations that care for their people perform better. You build on one cultural layer, three instruments and eight years of expertise in reintegration.”

Why stronger: “What you can build on / You build on” turns the foundation into something the visitor uses. The eight years of expertise stay as proof — now in service of them. (Keep the brand name in the section; it's the framing of the intro that matters.)

5

“References” section · card blurb

“We brought structure” — the reference talks about us

The card text describes what Montisoro did (“We brought structure, made the costs visible…”). In a reference the client is the hero; the strongest teaser shows what the client won, not what the firm did. (Same pattern on the References overview page — see section 7.)

Before

“From fragmented follow-up to one approach — We brought structure, made the costs visible and intervened sooner where it counted.”

After

“From fragmented follow-up to one approach — Structure restored, costs made visible, and faster intervention where it counted.”

Why stronger: without “we” as subject the line becomes a result the client achieved. The reader more easily projects that result onto their own organization — exactly what a reference should trigger.

6

“Contact” section (slot) + repeated on About

“Discover how Montisoro strengthens your approach”

The closing CTA opens on the brand. The visitor reads “Montisoro” first, only then “your approach”. This exact line also appears on the About page — fix it once, two places get stronger.

Before

“Discover how Montisoro strengthens your approach, from the first day of absence to sustainable return to work.”

After

“Discover how you strengthen your approach, from the first day of absence to sustainable return to work.”

Why stronger: the visitor becomes the actor of their own improvement. Shorter distance between reader and promise; the brand name is redundant here (the whole page is already Montisoro).

Already strong — leave unchanged

Hero opening line (“Every day an employee is absent costs up to €266”) leads with the visitor's pain. Calculator block (“Slide in your salary cost … see what you can save”) is exemplary customer-focus. The four figures (2,500 cases, €14.7M, 30–35%, 92–100K) are proof/authority — the brand focus is right here. Founder section is an origin story and may stay in the first-person/brand voice; it builds empathy. “Are we a match? →” invites the visitor — strong.

2 · Approach

7.5 / 10 Excellent problem sections in the visitor's voice — a couple of “we-do” lines in the statement and bridge blocks keep the score under 9.
7

Hero · subtitle

“Montisoro makes visible … and builds the structure”

The headline (“Absence is not the problem. It is the signal.”) is beautifully customer-focused. The subtitle then falls back on Montisoro as the subject of two verbs.

Before

“Montisoro makes visible where things get stuck and builds the structure to enable a lasting return to work.”

After

“You see where things get stuck, and gain the structure to enable a lasting return to work.”

Why stronger: “You see … and gain” puts the visitor in the driver's seat of their own insight. The promise feels like something they acquire, not a service description.

8

“Statement” section · eyebrow + intro

“What we do — Montisoro makes care workable”

The eyebrow (“What we do”) and opening line (“Montisoro brings knowledge, structure and technology together…”) are brand-as-subject. What's strong is that the follow-up (“capability that stays in your organization”) is already customer-focused — the opening just doesn't yet match it.

Before

Eyebrow: “What we do” — “Montisoro brings knowledge, structure and technology together for a lasting return to work.”

After

Eyebrow: “What's in it for you” — “Knowledge, structure and technology come together into one system for a lasting return to work in your organization.”

Why stronger: the eyebrow now promises a gain instead of a service description, and the intro lines up with the already-strong “stays in your organization” further down. A consistent visitor voice throughout the block.

9

“ROI bridge” section · caption

“Our calculator makes visible”

Small but it counts: “Our calculator” centres the tool (and the brand), whereas the gain for the visitor (seeing for themselves what stays hidden) should be the trigger.

Before

“Our calculator makes visible what today often stays hidden.”

After

“See for yourself, in two minutes, what today often stays hidden.”

Why stronger: “See for yourself” is a direct, active invitation (agency with the visitor) and lowers the threshold with a time promise — which raises click-through to the calculator.

Already strong — leave unchanged

“The problem — The costliest absence cost? What you don't see.” is sharply customer-focused. The 10 signals (“For us, cases only become visible…”, “Our HR team…”) are deliberately in the visitor's own voice — that's recognition/self-diagnosis and exactly right; don't flip it. Closing “We'll look at where your organization is losing grip” is a balanced, collaborative “we”. Eyebrow “Our approach” is a navigation label and acceptable.

3 · About

8.0 / 10 An About page is allowed to be about the brand — here even with a best-in-class example of customer focus. Only the shared closing CTA and the mission opening are worth lifting.

Context: the one page where “we” is the norm

Visitors come to About to judge whether they trust the brand — here the brand voice is expected and appropriate. The bar shifts: not “avoid we”, but “tie every brand claim to the visitor”. This page already does that for the most part.

A model for the whole site

Principle 02 — “Ownership — We measure our success not by your dependence, but by your independence.” This is exactly the right move: a brand value fully translated into the client's gain. Use this line as the benchmark for every rewrite elsewhere.

10

“Mission” section · intro

Strong mission, but it opens on the brand

“Montisoro builds the knowledge, structure and technology that lets organisations see earlier…” — the gain for the organization is there, but only after the brand as builder. A small tilt puts the organization first.

Before

“Montisoro builds the knowledge, structure and technology that lets organisations see earlier what is breaking down, act better when things get complex and embed knowledge more sustainably.”

After

“With the right knowledge, structure and technology you see earlier what is breaking down, act better when things get complex and embed knowledge more sustainably. That's what Montisoro builds.”

Why stronger: the visitor (“you see / act / embed”) comes first; the brand claim closes as a signature. Same content, but the gain lands first — and sticks.

The closing CTA “Discover how Montisoro strengthens your approach” appears here too — see finding 6 (Home) for the rewrite; one fix covers both pages.

Already strong — leave unchanged

Hero (“Some companies are born from an opportunity. Montisoro from a conviction.”) — the brand voice is appropriate and distinctive here. Team bios and the origin story belong in the brand voice and build trust. The five principles are sharp; principle 02 is a site-wide model.

4 · Technology

7.5 / 10 A product page logically describes the product — and Casey + the waitlist already do so with the visitor in view. The hero and the Storm opening are the pivots.
11

Hero · subtitle

“Montisoro builds technology for organizations where…”

The hero opens on the brand as builder. The audience is named (“organizations where absence … is daily reality”), but in the third person — the visitor reads about “organizations”, not about themselves.

Before

“Montisoro builds technology for organizations where absence, employability and human complexity are daily reality.”

After

“For organizations like yours — where absence, employability and human complexity are daily reality — this is built.”

Why stronger: “like yours” pulls the visitor into the sentence and mirrors their reality back. The emphasis shifts from who builds it to who it exists for.

12

“Storm” section · opening

Opens on product history, not on what it gives you

“Storm has been Montisoro's SaaS platform since 2023. It brings information, roles, communication and follow-up together…” — brand and date first, then the function. What's strong is the closer (“makes organizations more consistent in how they act”); that gain can move forward.

Before

“Storm has been Montisoro's SaaS platform since 2023. It brings information, roles, communication and follow-up together in one environment.”

After

“All your information, roles, communication and follow-up in one environment. That's Storm — the SaaS platform corporate organizations have been building on since 2023.”

Why stronger: the sentence opens on what the visitor gets (“All your … in one environment”); “since 2023” and “corporate organizations” stay as proof — exactly where the brand focus is credible.

Already strong — leave unchanged

Casey AI (“learns from your context … grows with your organization”) is already customer-focused. Human in the loop (“People keep the final responsibility”) reassures the visitor. Waitlist (“Casey learns every day. So does your organization.”) and the closing (“We'll show you … what Casey can mean for your organization”) balance brand and visitor well.

5 · Calculator

9.5 / 10 A model example. Nothing to change — use it as the benchmark for the rest of the site.
Already strong — keep as benchmark

Hero: “What does absence really cost? In six short steps, based on your organisation … you get one grounded figure and your full report.” Report gate: “Receive your full absence report … Your report includes: Your total absence cost … What you can save by acting earlier … Your position versus the sector.” Every line is the visitor. The proof (“2,500+ cases · 8 years of practice”) sits as reassurance, not boasting. This is 9.5 copy.

6 · Fit Check

9.5 / 10 Entirely in the visitor's voice. Nothing to change.
Already strong — leave unchanged

Hero: “Is your absence approach ready for what organizations need today? Four questions. Two minutes. One smart match — between your organization and the approach that's truly needed today.” The four steps (Absence type, Need, Maturity, Scale) diagnose the visitor. “No fluffy HR test. A capability check.” sets the expectation sharply. Exemplary.

7 · References — overview

7.0 / 10 The hero is fine; the card teasers tell what “we” did instead of what the client won.
13

“The cases” section · card teasers

“We brought structure” in the teasers

The same teasers as on Home. In a reference, the client is the hero. “We brought structure…” makes Montisoro the subject; the reader projects more easily when the line describes the result at the client. The section title (“Every sector. One system.”) and “See how it played out” are strong.

Before

Volvo: “We brought structure, made the costs visible and intervened sooner where it counted.”

After

Volvo: “Structure restored, costs made visible and faster intervention where it counts — across multiple shifts.”

Why stronger: result phrasing without “we” lets every prospect recognise themselves in the outcome. The added context (“across multiple shifts”) makes it concrete and relatable.

Already strong — leave unchanged

Hero (“Clients who prove it … helps organisations strengthen lasting employability”) and “See how it played out” are good. Focus only on the teaser lines.

8 · Case detail (template)

7.0 / 10 Results and quotes are already in the client's voice (strong). The hero summary consistently makes Montisoro the subject — that's where the gain sits, and one fix in the template improves every case.
14

Every case · hero summary (field hero_sum in cases.js)

Make the client the hero, not Montisoro

In all four cases Montisoro is the grammatical subject of the summary: “Montisoro brought absence under control”, “Montisoro follows up employees”, “Montisoro built a health policy”. In a case the CEO-reader wants to recognise themselves in the client — the client has to play the lead, with Montisoro as the guide that made it possible.

Before · Volvo

“In an industrial, multi-shift environment, Montisoro brought absence back under control, not with more meetings, but with one system that unites insight, structure and follow-through.”

After · Volvo

“In an industrial, multi-shift environment, Volvo Group brought absence back under control — not with more meetings, but with one system that unites insight, structure and follow-through. Montisoro helped build it.”

Before · Alcon

“…Montisoro personally follows up absent employees through its Re-boarding programme.”

After · Alcon

“…Alcon keeps absent employees personally on board through Montisoro's Re-boarding programme.”

Why stronger: the client achieves the result; Montisoro is the guide that makes it possible. That's precisely the StoryBrand effect that makes references persuasive — the prospect sees themselves in the lead. One change to the hero_sum pattern (cases.js) propagates to every case and every future case from the dashboard.

Already strong — leave unchanged

Result cards (“More clarity”, “Long-term absence down sharply”, “Faster follow-up”) are in client outcomes — exactly right. Client quotes (William De Plecker, Hilde Hermans) are authentic social proof in the client's voice. “At a glance” (Challenge/Approach/Result/Duration) is neutral and scannable. Titles like “What the collaboration delivered” are result-focused — keep them.

9 · FAQ

8.5 / 10 Questions and answers are largely in the visitor's voice. Hardly anything to do.
Already strong — leave unchanged

The questions are posed in the visitor's voice (“How quickly do we see results?”, “Does this work for our sector or SME?”) — that's self-identification and right. The answers balance well: “You bring your situation, we listen”, “After the first conversation you receive a concrete proposal … grounded in your own figures”, “we tailor the approach to your reality”. The “we” here is always the guide beside the client — appropriate.

10 · Contact

9.0 / 10 Honest, pressure-free and you-first. Nothing structural to change.
Already strong — leave unchanged

Hero: “Choose how you reach us. No sales pitch … You bring your question, we listen, and tell you honestly whether we can add value.” Channels are framed around the visitor's choice. “You speak directly with the founder” and “Your data stays with us” build trust. A strong piece of guide copy — the honesty (“If we're not the right party, we'll say so”) raises credibility.

Shared CTAs & footer

8.5 / 10 The primary buttons are already action- and visitor-focused; one label is worth lifting.
15

Secondary CTA · multiple pages

“Explore our approach first” / “Discover our approach first”

Small detail, high frequency: “our approach” makes the brand the owner. For a navigation button the approach itself is enough; “our” adds nothing but brand focus.

Before

“Explore our approach first →” · “Discover our approach first →”

After

“Explore the approach first →” · “Here's how we do it →”

Why stronger: more neutral and more action-oriented; the button promises content, not brand ownership. (“Here's how we do it” keeps the guide voice without the possessive “our”.)

Already strong — leave unchanged

“Calculate your absence cost →” (possessive-correct: it's your cost), “Book a call →” (clear action), “Are we a match? →”, “All references →”. The footer is navigation/legal and neutral — no we-problem.

Prioritization — how you reach 9.5

The site already sits at 7.9. These six moves carry the greatest effect and together touch barely ten sentences:

  1. Flip the section headings (Home “Why Montisoro” + “The Montisoro Foundation”; Approach “What we do”). Headings weigh most — they set the first impression per section. → findings 2, 4, 8
  2. Tilt the hero subtitles toward “you see / you gain” (Home, Approach, Technology). → findings 1, 7, 11
  3. Case summaries: client as subject in the hero_sum pattern (cases.js) — one fix, all cases. → finding 14
  4. Reference teasers from “we did” to “result at the client” (Home + overview). → findings 5, 13
  5. Shared closing CTA “Discover how Montisoro strengthens your approach” → “…how you strengthen your approach” (Home + About in one go). → finding 6
  6. Micro-labels: “our calculator/approach” → neutral-active. → findings 9, 15

Leave Calculator, Fit Check, Contact, the numbers/proof blocks, the Founder and team sections and the client quotes untouched — those carry the brand voice where it belongs (authority and empathy) and are already strong.
